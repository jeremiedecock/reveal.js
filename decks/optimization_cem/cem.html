Cross-Entropy Method


Maintains an explicit probability distribution over the solution space

  • Called a proposal distribution $P_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$
  • Used to propose new samples for the next generation
  • Parameterized by $\boldsymbol{\theta}$
  • The family of probability distributions is chosen according to the objective function landscape

Usually: a multivariate normal distribution with $\boldsymbol{\theta} = (\boldsymbol{\mu}, \ms{\Sigma})$

Choix courant : une loi normale multivariée paramétrée par un vecteur moyenne 𝛍 et une matrice de covariance 𝚺
Common choice: a multivariate normal distribution parameterized by a mean vector 𝛍 and a covariance matrix 𝚺

The proposal distribution $P_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$

In the case of the multivariate normal distribution, parameters are updated according to the maximum likelihood estimate:

$$\boldsymbol{\mu}^{(k+1)} = \frac{1}{m_{\text{elite}}} \sum_{i=1}^{m_{\text{elite}}} \boldsymbol{x}^{(i)}$$ $$\ms{\Sigma}^{(k+1)} = \frac{1}{m_{\text{elite}}} \sum_{i=1}^{m_{\text{elite}}} \left( \boldsymbol{x}^{(i)} - \boldsymbol{\mu}^{(k+1)} \right) \left( \boldsymbol{x}^{(i)} - \boldsymbol{\mu}^{(k+1)} \right)^{\top}$$

with:
$k$ = the iteration index, so that $\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(k+1)} = (\boldsymbol{\mu}^{(k+1)}, \ms{\Sigma}^{(k+1)})$
$m_{\text{elite}}$ = the number of “elite” (i.e. best) samples
$\boldsymbol{x}^{(1)}, \dots, \boldsymbol{x}^{(m_{\text{elite}})}$ = the elite samples of iteration $k$

La mise à jour est l'estimateur du maximum de vraisemblance calculé sur les seuls échantillons élites : moyenne empirique et covariance empirique.
The update is the maximum likelihood estimate computed on the elite samples only: empirical mean and empirical covariance.

Why “cross-entropy”?

Fitting $P_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$ to the elite samples by maximum likelihood is equivalent to minimizing the cross-entropy $H$ between the empirical distribution $\hat{p}_{\text{elite}}$ of the elite samples and $P_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$:

$$\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(k+1)} = \arg\min_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \; H(\hat{p}_{\text{elite}}, P_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}) = \arg\max_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \; \frac{1}{m_{\text{elite}}} \sum_{i=1}^{m_{\text{elite}}} \log P_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}(\boldsymbol{x}^{(i)})$$

Historically, the method comes from rare-event simulation: $P_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$ iteratively approaches the optimal importance-sampling distribution — the one concentrated on the event of interest (here: sampling low values of $f$).

Minimiser la cross-entropy H(p̂, P_θ) = −E_p̂[log P_θ] revient exactement à maximiser la log-vraisemblance des échantillons élites : c'est l'étape « fit » de l'algorithme, d'où le nom de la méthode.
Minimizing the cross-entropy H(p̂, P_θ) = −E_p̂[log P_θ] is exactly maximizing the log-likelihood of the elite samples: this is the “fit” step of the algorithm, hence the method's name.

La famille de distributions doit être assez flexible pour capturer les caractéristiques pertinentes de la fonction objectif. Ici on montre les limites d'une loi normale sur une fonction objectif multimodale : elle place l'essentiel de sa densité entre les deux minima, alors qu'un modèle de mélange peut se centrer sur chacun d'eux.
The distribution family should be flexible enough to capture the relevant features of the objective function. Here we show the limitations of using a normal distribution on a multimodal objective function, which assigns greater density in between the two minima. A mixture model is able to center itself over each minimum. The normal distribution is unable to capture multiple local minima, in contrast to mixture models which can maintain several.

Cross-Entropy Method

Strengths:

  • Simplicity
  • Fast convergence on many practical problems

Weaknesses:

  • Premature convergence: the variance can collapse too early (hence the noise added in the “noisy” CEM below)
  • Scales poorly to high dimensions ($\ms{\Sigma}$ has $O(n^2)$ parameters)
  • Sensitive to the choice of the distribution family and of $m$, $m_{\text{elite}}$

Example:
I. Szita and A. Lőrincz, Learning Tetris Using the Noisy Cross-Entropy Method (2006), Neural Computation, 18(12), pp.2936–2941 [DOI]

Le CEM « bruité » de Szita & Lőrincz ajoute du bruit à la covariance pour éviter la convergence prématurée — c'est l'exemple d'application le plus connu (Tetris).
The “noisy” CEM of Szita & Lőrincz adds noise to the covariance to avoid premature convergence — the best-known application example (Tetris).
