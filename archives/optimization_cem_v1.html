Cross-Entropy Method

Cross-Entropy Method


Maintains an explicit probability distribution over the solution space

  • Called a proposal distribution $P_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$
  • Used to propose new samples for the next generation
  • Parametrized by $\boldsymbol{\theta}$
  • The family of probability distribution is chosen according to the objective function landscape

Usually : multivariate Normal distributions $\boldsymbol{\theta} = (\boldsymbol{\mu}, \ms{\Sigma})$

Common choice : multivariate Normal distributions parameterized by a mean vector 𝛍 and a covariance matrix 𝚺

Cross-Entropy Method

In the case of the multivariate normal distribution, parameters are updated according to the maximum likelihood estimate:

$$\boldsymbol{\mu}^{(k+1)} = \frac{1}{m_{\text{elite}}} \sum_{i=1}^{m_{\text{elite}}} \boldsymbol{x}^{(i)}$$ $$\ms{\Sigma}^{(k+1)} = \frac{1}{m_{\text{elite}}} \sum_{i=1}^{m_{\text{elite}}} \left( \boldsymbol{x}^{(i)} - \boldsymbol{\mu}^{(k+1)} \right) \left( \boldsymbol{x}^{(i)} - \boldsymbol{\mu}^{(k+1)} \right)^{\top}$$

with:
$k$ = the iteration index
$m_{\text{elite}}$ = the number of “elite” (i.e. best) samples

Cross-Entropy Method

Multivariate Normal distributions is not always the right family of distributions to choose…

Here, a mixture model would be a better choice…

The distribution family should be flexible enough to capture the relevant features of the objective function. Here we show the limitations of using a normal distribution on a multimodal objective function, which assigns greater density in between the two minima. A mixture model is able to center itself over each minimum. The normal distribution is unable to capture multiple local minima, in contrast to mixture models which can maintain several.
